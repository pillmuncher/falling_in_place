* Perceptual

** Topological Immersion
Loading a problem through intense focus until its structure becomes
visible as topology rather than symbol or geometry. Requires
uninterrupted time. Cannot be rushed.

** Sleeping On It
After sufficient immersion, the unconscious ranges over the loaded
structure without the constraints of deliberate logical stepping.
The topology becomes visible when you stop trying to see it.
Poincaré and Hadamard document the same phenomenon.

* Reframing

** What If It Were Different?
Wittgenstein's frame-destabilizer. Prevents the current framing
from hardening into the only possible framing. Does not require
knowing the answer — only keeping the question open.

** The Zhuangzi Rejection
Refusing imported rightness criteria rather than arguing within
them. Not a refutation — a declination. Huizi's epistemology is
not wrong, it is simply not the relevant world.

** Restoring Context-Dependency
Before applying a rightness criterion, ask: which world are we in?
Common wisdom is often right in some worlds and wrong in others.
The interesting question is always which world you are currently in.

* Algebraic

** Trusting Pre-Verbal Intuition
The structure can be seen before it can be named. The name arrives
later and confirms what was already there. The intuition does not
have to be perfect — close enough for the vocabulary to take over.

** Retroactive Vocabulary
Learning the formal vocabulary for something you have already been
doing intuitively. The vocabulary is load-bearing: it makes certain
bugs visible as bugs and certain properties obligatory.

* Diagnostic

** Wart vs. Scar
A wart is unexplained. A scar tells you exactly what happened and
why. The right abstraction doesn't always simplify — sometimes it
just clarifies. Locating an anomaly in its right world turns warts
into scars.

** Detecting Smuggling
Noticing when a rightness criterion has crossed a world boundary
uninvited. The tell: a concept that works perfectly in one domain
is applied to another domain where its grounding hasn't been
established. Examples: moral truth, Prolog's cut, designed-in
extensibility.
